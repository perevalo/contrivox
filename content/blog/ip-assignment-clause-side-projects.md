# IP Assignment Clauses: Does Your Employer Own Your Side Projects?

Tom had been a backend developer at a logistics startup for two years when he decided to build something on his own. He spent six months working nights and weekends on a personal finance app — his own idea, his own computer, his own time. It had nothing to do with logistics software.

When he told his employer he was releasing it publicly, their legal team sent him a letter. They claimed his employment agreement's IP assignment clause gave them ownership of the app.

Tom went back and read his contract. The clause said: *"Employee hereby assigns to Company all inventions, developments, software, and work product conceived, developed, or reduced to practice by Employee during the term of employment, whether or not using Company resources, that relate to the Company's current or reasonably anticipated business."*

The startup had recently started exploring "financial tools for small business customers." His app, they argued, related to that.

This is not a hypothetical edge case. Disputes like this happen regularly — and developers, designers, writers, and engineers lose them.

> **Quick answer:** A broad IP assignment clause can give your employer ownership of work you create entirely on your own time, with your own equipment, if it can be argued to "relate" to the company's business. Several states limit this — but most don't. If you have side projects, you need to understand your contract before you're in Tom's situation.

## What an IP Assignment Clause Actually Says

Most employment contracts contain [intellectual property assignment](/clauses/ip-assignment-clause) language. At its most basic, it reads something like:

*"All work product, inventions, and developments created by Employee in connection with their employment are the sole property of Company."*

That's reasonable. Work you do on company time, using company resources, for company purposes belongs to the company. No one seriously disputes that.

The problem is the modifier language that follows in many contracts:

*"...including any work product created during the term of employment that relates to the Company's business, products, research, or development, whether or not created using Company resources or during work hours."*

That addition changes everything. "Relates to" is doing enormous work in that sentence, and it's intentionally vague.

---

## Work Made for Hire vs. Assignment of Inventions — The Difference

These are two distinct legal concepts that often get conflated:

**[Work made for hire](/clauses/work-made-for-hire-clause)** applies to work created within the scope of employment — work you were specifically hired or directed to create. Under copyright law, the employer automatically owns this without needing an assignment clause.

**Assignment of inventions** is different. It's a contractual transfer of ownership of things you create that weren't necessarily within the scope of your job — including side projects, personal apps, creative work, and inventions developed independently.

A standard work-for-hire clause is fine. An invention assignment clause that extends beyond your job scope — to your personal projects — is where you need to pay attention.

---

## How Broad Is "Related to the Company's Business"?

This is the phrase that ends careers and kills side projects.

Courts have interpreted "related to the company's business" broadly in many cases. A company in the software space could argue that virtually any software application you build "relates to" their business. A media company could claim your personal writing relates to their content business. A biotech company could argue your personal health-related side project relates to their research pipeline.

The risk grows as the company grows. A startup that pivoted into your side project's space after you joined — even after you started the project — may claim that your project now "relates to their reasonably anticipated business."

This is the clause that has claimed ownership of engineers' open-source projects, designers' freelance portfolios, and developers' personal apps. The stakes are real.

## The States That Protect You

Several states have enacted laws that limit how far employer IP claims can reach. If you live in one of these states, part of your IP assignment clause may be legally unenforceable — but only the part that goes beyond what state law permits.

**California** — [Labor Code Section 2870](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/codes_displaySection.xhtml?lawCode=LAB&sectionNum=2870) is the gold standard. It prohibits employers from claiming ownership of inventions that:
- Were developed entirely on the employee's own time
- Did not use employer equipment, supplies, facilities, or trade secret information
- Did not relate to the employer's business or actual and demonstrably anticipated research or development
- Did not result from work performed for the employer

Even in California, "relate to the employer's business" remains a gray area — but the statute at least provides a framework for challenging overly broad claims.

**Delaware, Illinois, Minnesota, North Carolina, and Washington** have similar statutes with comparable protections. The language varies by state, but the core principle is the same: work created entirely on your own time, without company resources, and unrelated to the employer's actual business belongs to you.

If you're not in one of these states, your employer's IP claim is limited mainly by contract language and the willingness of courts to interpret "related to the business" reasonably — which varies widely.

## How to Identify If Your Clause Is Too Broad

Read the IP assignment section of your employment contract and look for:

**The trigger for assignment:** Does it say "in the scope of employment," or does it extend to "during the term of employment"? The latter is much broader.

**The resource carve-out:** Does the clause still apply even if you use your own equipment and time? If yes, that's aggressive.

**The "relates to" definition:** Is "Company's business" defined? Vague definitions favor the employer.

**A statutory carve-out:** Some employment contracts, especially from companies with California employees, include an explicit carve-out referencing Labor Code Section 2870 or the equivalent. If yours does, you have more protection. If it doesn't, ask for one.

**Listed exclusions:** Some contracts require you to disclose side projects at the time of signing so they can be explicitly excluded. If you can do this, do it — get your personal projects listed and excluded by name.

---

## How to Negotiate a Carve-Out Before Signing

The best time to address an IP assignment clause is before you sign. This is not an unusual negotiation — engineers and developers raise it regularly, and many employers will accommodate reasonable requests.

**Ask for a side project exclusion addendum.** This is a short document attached to your employment agreement that explicitly excludes specified personal projects from the IP assignment. List the project by name, describe it briefly, and have both parties sign.

**Ask for a personal project policy.** Some companies have written policies on this. If yours does, ask for it in writing and make sure your understanding matches what the policy actually says.

**Propose specific language.** Instead of vague negotiation, propose adding: *"This assignment does not apply to inventions that qualify for exclusion under [your state's equivalent of California Labor Code Section 2870], specifically including work developed entirely on Employee's own time using non-Company resources and unrelated to Company's current business."*

**Be transparent.** If you have active side projects, disclose them upfront. It's far better to have an explicit written exclusion than to hope the clause won't apply later.

---

## What To Do If You Already Signed a Broad IP Clause

**First, assess your actual exposure.** Is your side project genuinely "related to" your employer's business as a reasonable person would define it? Or is it a stretch? The closer the overlap, the higher the risk.

**Second, stop using any company resources** — equipment, internet, time, or colleagues — for your personal project, from this point forward. Document when and where you work on it.

**Third, do not announce or release your project without legal advice** if there's meaningful overlap with your employer's business. An employment attorney familiar with IP assignment in your state can give you a real assessment of the risk before you're in Tom's position.

**Fourth, in your next contract negotiation,** make the IP assignment clause a priority before you sign.

## FAQ: IP Assignment Clauses

**Does an IP assignment clause apply to projects I started before I was hired?**
It shouldn't — and most contracts don't claim it does. But if the contract language is broad enough to cover "during the term of employment," some employers have tried to extend claims to pre-existing projects that were developed further after hiring. This is a red flag in the contract language.

**What if I never used my employer's equipment or time?**
In states with protective statutes (California, Illinois, etc.), this is a meaningful defense. In other states, it may or may not matter depending on the contract language and courts' interpretation of "related to."

**Can my employer claim ownership of open-source code I contribute to?**
If the open-source contributions relate to the company's business and were made during your employment, a broad IP assignment clause could theoretically apply. Many companies have explicit policies on open-source contribution — ask for yours in writing.

**Does IP assignment apply after I leave?**
The assignment clause covers inventions created *during* employment. After you leave, a former employer has no claim on new work — unless a separate post-employment IP clause exists (uncommon but not unheard of).

**What happens if my employer and I dispute who owns something?**
IP ownership disputes can escalate to litigation. Courts look at the contract language, the nature of the work, the state's law, and the facts of how and when the project was developed.
