# Is a Non-Compete Enforceable in New York?

David, a hedge fund analyst in Manhattan, had been at his firm for three years when a competing fund approached him. The offer was significant. His current employment agreement had a one-year non-compete covering all investment firms in New York, New Jersey, and Connecticut.

He'd heard that New York had passed a non-compete ban. He assumed he was in the clear.

He wasn't — at least not in the way he thought.

> **Quick answer:** New York's non-compete situation is genuinely complicated. The state legislature passed a near-total ban on non-competes in 2023, but Governor Hochul vetoed it in December 2023. Current New York law still enforces non-competes — but only if they pass a "reasonableness" test applied by courts. For senior professionals in finance, law, and media, enforcement is real.

## What Actually Happened With New York's Non-Compete Ban

New York came close to becoming the second state (after California) to largely ban non-competes. In June 2023, the state legislature passed a bill that would have prohibited non-compete agreements for most workers and limited them to senior executives above a significant salary threshold even then.

Governor Kathy Hochul vetoed it in December 2023.

Her stated concern: the bill's scope was too broad and would harm New York's ability to compete for business investment. She expressed interest in a narrower version — one focused on lower-wage workers — but no revised legislation has passed as of 2025.

The result: New York is back to its pre-bill status, enforcing non-competes under a judicially developed "reasonableness" standard. If you signed a non-compete with a New York employer, it may be enforceable — and the veto means the law that would have protected you doesn't exist yet.

## How New York Courts Evaluate Non-Competes

New York courts don't apply a simple checklist. They use a balancing test developed through decades of case law, asking whether the non-compete:

**1. Is no greater than required to protect the employer's legitimate interest**
New York recognizes two core legitimate interests: protection of trade secrets, and protection against unfair competition by someone who could significantly damage the business by departing. A legitimate interest is not just "we don't want competition" — it has to be something specific and real.

**2. Does not impose undue hardship on the employee**
A restriction that leaves someone unemployable in their field or geographic area weighs against enforcement.

**3. Does not harm the public**
Courts consider whether enforcing the restriction would deprive the public of important services — a concern that comes up most often in the healthcare and legal sectors.

**4. Has a reasonable duration**
New York courts have generally found 6–12 months reasonable. Restrictions of 24 months or longer face significant scrutiny. Multi-year restrictions are typically reserved for very senior roles with extraordinary access to sensitive information.

**5. Has a reasonable geographic scope**
A restriction covering only the area where you actually worked is more defensible than a nationwide or global ban. For most employees, a statewide restriction is at the outer edge of what courts typically enforce.

## Where Non-Competes Hit Hardest in New York

New York courts have historically been most willing to enforce non-competes in three industries:

**Finance:** Hedge funds, private equity firms, and investment banks operate in a world where personnel relationships, trading strategies, and client intelligence are closely guarded. Courts have been receptive to enforcing non-competes — and garden leave arrangements — for analysts, portfolio managers, and senior traders.

**Law:** Non-compete clauses for lawyers are actually prohibited by the New York Rules of Professional Conduct, which prioritizes client choice of counsel. But non-solicitation clauses are common and enforced.

**Media and entertainment:** Non-competes for on-air talent, senior editors, and key executives at media companies have a significant enforcement history in New York courts.

If you're in one of these sectors and you hold a senior role with client or audience relationships, your non-compete is in a higher-risk category.

## Garden Leave: What It Is and Why You Should Ask for It

[Garden leave](/clauses/garden-leave-clause) is an alternative to a traditional non-compete that's gaining traction in New York — partly because courts view it more favorably, and partly because it's a more defensible arrangement.

Under a garden leave clause, when you leave your job, your employer keeps paying your full salary and benefits for the duration of the restricted period. You're bound not to work for a competitor — but you're being compensated for that restriction.

This is materially different from a standard non-compete, which restricts you without any payment. Courts in New York and elsewhere tend to view garden leave arrangements as inherently more reasonable, because the employee is being genuinely compensated for what they're giving up.

If you're negotiating a new employment contract in New York — especially in finance — asking for garden leave in place of, or alongside, a traditional non-compete is worth pursuing. It costs you nothing to ask, and it changes the nature of the restriction significantly if you leave.

## What NYC Workers Specifically Should Know

The Freelance Isn't Free Act and various New York City-specific labor laws offer additional protections for certain workers — but they don't directly address non-competes. For employees working in New York City, state law applies.

One practical note for NYC-based workers: the geographic scope of "New York City" alone in a non-compete restriction is generally treated as reasonable by courts, given the concentration of competing firms within that geography. A restriction that's limited to NYC is harder to challenge than one covering the tri-state area or the entire US.

## What To Do If Your New York Non-Compete Seems Aggressive

**Negotiate before signing.** New York courts scrutinize reasonableness, which means there's a logic to negotiating scope down before you're bound. Push for a shorter duration (12 months rather than 24), a narrower geographic scope (metro area rather than statewide), and limiting the restriction to roles where you'd actually be using confidential information.

**Ask about garden leave.** Especially in finance — frame it as an alternative rather than a demand: "I'd be comfortable with a non-compete if it includes full salary continuation during the restricted period."

**Document what consideration you're receiving.** New York courts require that a non-compete be supported by meaningful consideration. Simply being offered a job is typically enough at the start of employment. A mid-employment non-compete presented without any additional benefit — no bonus, no raise, no new access — is more vulnerable.

**If you're already in this situation,** consult a New York employment attorney before making any moves. The factors that determine enforceability in your specific case depend on your role, your industry, what you had access to, and the exact language of your agreement.

## FAQ: Non-Competes in New York

**Is New York going to ban non-competes?**
It remains a live legislative issue. Governor Hochul vetoed the 2023 bill but indicated openness to a narrower version. As of 2025, no new bill has passed. The situation could change — but you should evaluate your contract under current law.

**Can my New York employer enforce a non-compete if I'm laid off?**
New York courts have generally held that a layoff doesn't automatically void a non-compete — though courts may be less sympathetic to enforcement when the employer ended the relationship. The specific facts matter.

**What if my non-compete has a New York choice-of-law clause but I move to California?**
California courts will generally refuse to enforce the non-compete regardless of what the contract says. But if your former employer sues you in New York, a New York court may apply New York law.

**Does New York blue-pencil non-competes like Texas does?**
New York courts can modify overly broad restrictions rather than void them entirely, but they're not required to in the way Texas courts are. New York is more likely than Texas to find an overly broad restriction simply unenforceable.

**My non-compete says I can't work for "any financial firm in North America for three years." Is that enforceable?**
Almost certainly not as written. A three-year, continent-wide restriction is far outside what New York courts have historically enforced. But a court might rewrite it rather than eliminate it entirely — don't assume it gives you a free pass.
