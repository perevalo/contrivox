# Is a Non-Compete Agreement Enforceable? A Plain-English State Guide

**Quick summary:** Whether your non-compete is enforceable depends almost entirely on which state's law applies. California, Minnesota, North Dakota, and Oklahoma ban them. Most other states enforce them — but only if they pass a three-part reasonableness test covering duration, geography, and scope. There is no federal rule. The FTC's 2024 ban was struck down by a court.

## The Three-Part Test Most States Apply

States that enforce non-competes don't enforce every non-compete. Courts apply a reasonableness test with three components:

**1. Is there a legitimate business interest to protect?**
Courts won't enforce a non-compete just because an employer prefers less competition. There has to be something real at stake: trade secrets, confidential customer relationships, specialized training the employer paid for, or proprietary processes the employee had access to.

An employee who spent three years on a company's product development roadmap has legitimate exposure. A cashier who knows the store's hours does not.

**2. Is the restriction reasonable in scope?**
The restriction must be proportionate to the interest being protected. Courts examine:

- **Duration:** 6–12 months is generally enforceable across most states. 24 months is the upper edge in most jurisdictions. Anything beyond 2 years draws heavy scrutiny.
- **Geography:** Restricted area must reflect where the employer actually operates and where the employee actually worked. A nationwide restriction for a regional sales rep is often seen as overreach.
- **Activity:** Must target actual competitive activity, not every conceivable job in the same broad industry.

**3. Is it supported by adequate consideration?**
A contract needs something of value exchanged by both sides. An initial job offer counts as consideration. A non-compete handed to an existing employee mid-employment — with nothing offered in return — can fail for lack of consideration in some states.

## State-by-State Enforceability

| State | Status | Key Rules |
|---|---|---|
| **California** | Banned | Bus. & Prof. Code § 16600. SB 699 (2024) voids out-of-state non-competes for California workers. |
| **Minnesota** | Banned | Minn. Stat. § 181.988, effective July 1, 2023. |
| **North Dakota** | Banned | N.D. Cent. Code § 9-08-06. |
| **Oklahoma** | Largely banned | Okla. Stat. tit. 15, § 219A. |
| **Florida** | Actively enforced | Fla. Stat. § 542.335. Burden on employee to prove unreasonable. Blue-penciling allowed. |
| **Texas** | Enforced with blue-penciling | Tex. Bus. & Com. Code § 15.50. Courts rewrite overbroad clauses rather than voiding. |
| **New York** | Limited enforcement | Reasonableness test applies strictly. Courts increasingly reluctant to enforce. |
| **Illinois** | Partially restricted | IFSA (2022): non-competes require $75k+ salary; non-solicitations require $45k+. |
| **Georgia** | Enforced | Ga. Code Ann. § 13-8-50 et seq. Courts blue-pencil; 2-year max generally enforced. |
| **Virginia** | Restricted | Non-competes for low-wage workers (below average weekly wage) void since 2020. |
| **Washington** | Restricted | Non-competes for workers earning under $120,559 (2024 threshold) void. |
| **Colorado** | Restricted | Non-competes only for workers earning 60%+ above state minimum wage; limited to protecting trade secrets. |

## What Makes a Non-Compete Fail the Test

Even in enforcement states, courts regularly decline to enforce non-competes that are:

- **Worldwide in geographic scope** for an employee who worked regionally
- **3+ years in duration** with no clear justification
- **Written to cover any job in the industry**, not just work that actually uses confidential information
- **Presented to existing employees** without new compensation or benefits at signing
- **Part of a contract where the employer breached first** (e.g., didn't pay what they owed)

Courts in blue-penciling states (Texas, Florida, Georgia) won't void the whole clause — they'll cut it down to what they consider reasonable and enforce that version. This is actually a risk: you can't assume an aggressive clause will be thrown out entirely; you may just get a narrower restriction that a court still enforces.

## The Governing Law Clause Matters

Your non-compete says it's governed by Texas law, but you work in California. Which state controls?

California courts will generally apply California law to protect California workers, regardless of what the contract says. Most other states will apply the law specified in the contract — unless doing so would be contrary to a fundamental public policy of the employee's home state.

If you've moved states since signing, or if your employer and work location are in different states, the governing law question matters a great deal. This is one scenario where a quick attorney consult pays off.

## FAQ: Non-Compete Enforceability

**Is my non-compete automatically void if I'm in California?**
Almost certainly yes for an employment non-compete. California courts void these under Business and Professions Code § 16600, and since SB 699 (effective January 2024), they won't enforce non-competes signed under another state's law either.

**Does the FTC ban protect me?**
No. The FTC's 2024 non-compete rule was vacated by a federal court in August 2024. See our detailed breakdown: [The FTC Non-Compete Ban](/blog/ftc-non-compete-ban).

**Can my employer enforce a non-compete even if it's too broad?**
In blue-penciling states (Texas, Florida, Georgia, others), yes — the court will narrow the restriction to something reasonable and enforce that. Don't assume a broad clause is unenforceable just because it looks aggressive.

**What if I was laid off — does the non-compete still apply?**
It depends on the state and the contract. Some states (not the majority) limit enforcement when the employer initiates termination without cause. Texas generally enforces regardless. California prohibits enforcement regardless.
