# A NDA or An NDA? The Correct Grammar for Your Legal Documents

**Quick summary:** The correct phrase is **an NDA**, not **a NDA**, because “NDA” is pronounced “en-dee-ay,” and article choice depends on sound. When deciding between **a nda or an nda** in legal documents, emails, or contracts, choose **an NDA** for standard professional English.

---

A tiny article can make a contract look polished—or distract from it. Non-disclosure agreements are common in hiring, vendor onboarding, investor conversations, and product partnerships, so the wording around them matters. If you are unsure whether to write **a NDA** or **an NDA**, here is the rule and when to use each phrase.

## Main Section 1

### The grammar rule: choose the article based on sound

The choice between **a** and **an** depends on the sound that follows, not the first written letter. Use **a** before a consonant sound, such as **a contract**, **a vendor**, or **a non-disclosure agreement**. Use **an** before a vowel sound, such as **an agreement**, **an employee**, or **an NDA**.

Because **NDA** is pronounced letter by letter—**“en-dee-ay”**—it begins with a vowel sound. That makes **an NDA** the correct form.

### Examples you can copy into legal documents

Use **an NDA** when the acronym appears by itself:

- Please send **an NDA** before we discuss the pilot.
- The employee signed **an NDA** on their first day.
- We need **an NDA** covering trade secrets, customer data, and product roadmaps.
- Could you review **an NDA** before we share confidential pricing?
- The contractor requested **an NDA** before the discovery call.

Use **a non-disclosure agreement** when you write out the full phrase:

- Please send **a non-disclosure agreement** for review.
- The company requires **a non-disclosure agreement** before sharing technical materials.
- We signed **a non-disclosure agreement** to protect the launch plan.

Both versions can be correct, but they follow different pronunciation rules. **An NDA** works because “N” sounds like “en.” **A non-disclosure agreement** works because “non” begins with a consonant sound.

### When “a NDA” might appear

You may see **a NDA** in informal writing, search queries, or AI-generated content, but it is not the standard form in professional legal writing. It would only make sense if someone pronounced “NDA” as a word, which is rare and not typical.

For legal documents, employment paperwork, vendor contracts, investor materials, and business emails, use **an NDA**.

### Full phrase vs. acronym in contracts

In a formal contract, the best practice is to spell out the term first and define it. For example:

> This Non-Disclosure Agreement (the “Agreement”) is entered into as of June 9, 2026, by and between the parties listed below.

After the term is defined, the document may refer to “the Agreement” rather than repeating **NDA** throughout. In emails or summaries, however, **an NDA** is perfectly acceptable.

## Main Section 2

### Using NDA correctly in employment documents

NDAs are especially common in employment because companies often need to protect confidential information, customer lists, pricing, product plans, and internal processes. In employment contexts, you may see phrases like **an employment NDA**, **an employee NDA**, or **an onboarding NDA** because “employment,” “employee,” and “onboarding” begin with vowel sounds.

Examples:

- **An employment NDA** may restrict how an employee handles confidential information.
- **An employee NDA** should clearly explain what information is protected.
- **An onboarding NDA** is often signed with other employment documents.

But if another word comes before NDA, the article may change:

- **A new employee NDA** was added to the onboarding packet.
- **A standard NDA** is used for all contractors.
- **A mutual NDA** protects both parties.

The article depends on the word immediately after it.

### Using NDA correctly in emails and business communications

In business emails, clarity matters more than sounding overly formal. If you are asking someone to send or review a non-disclosure agreement, either of these is correct:

- Please send **an NDA** before we share the prototype.
- Please send **a non-disclosure agreement** before we share the prototype.

The first option is shorter and natural for readers who know the acronym. The second option is clearer for readers who may not be familiar with legal terminology.

### Common NDA grammar mistakes to avoid

| Phrase | Correct? | Why |
|---|---:|---|
| **an NDA** | Yes | “NDA” starts with the vowel sound “en.” |
| **a NDA** | No | Standard usage treats NDA as an initialism. |
| **a non-disclosure agreement** | Yes | “Non” starts with a consonant sound. |
| **an non-disclosure agreement** | No | “Non” does not start with a vowel sound. |
| **an employee NDA** | Yes | “Employee” starts with a vowel sound. |
| **a one-way NDA** | Yes | “One” is pronounced with a “w” sound. |

### Why grammar matters in legal documents

Correct grammar does not automatically make an NDA enforceable, but it helps your document look professional and reduces avoidable confusion. Sloppy wording can distract readers, weaken first impressions, or create ambiguity in surrounding sentences.

More importantly, the substance of the NDA matters. Before signing, review the scope of confidential information, exclusions, duration, permitted disclosures, return or destruction obligations, remedies, governing law, and any penalties for breach.

## FAQ: A NDA or An NDA? The Correct Grammar for Your Legal Documents

**Is it a NDA or an NDA?**

It is **an NDA**. The acronym NDA is pronounced “en-dee-ay,” so it begins with a vowel sound.

**Why do we say an NDA instead of a NDA?**

We say **an NDA** because the choice between **a** and **an** depends on sound. Since the letter “N” sounds like “en,” the correct article is **an**.

**Should I write “NDA” or “non-disclosure agreement” in a contract?**

In a formal contract, write out **Non-Disclosure Agreement** first and define it, such as “the Agreement.” In emails, summaries, and casual references, **an NDA** is correct and easy to read.
